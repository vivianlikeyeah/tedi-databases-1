Keys

A key is a value that uniquely identifies a record in a table. A key could be a single column of a combination of several columns.
Keys allow us to access data in a database. They also allow us to create relationships between tables.
Consider the following table: You have a database (table) that contains every students grades for the year. The table has a bunch of columns (fields) including StudentID, Year, Gender and Grade. A TEDI London staff members needs to access a students grade.

So they approach the student services administration and ask the following: “I need to know the grade of a student in my third year class.” “They’re a female student” “Their student ID is 1257” When the staff searches for all third year students, they aren't able to find the grade.

When the staff member then searches for all female students, they still arent able to track down the specific student.

However once the staff member enters the student's ID, they are able to identify them immediately. This field uniquely identifies each student, and is therefore a key.

Primary, Composite and Foreign Keys

Primary Keys

A primary key is a field that uniquely identifies a record. In the example above, the key for Customer Details is CustomerID and the key for Purchases is PurchaseID.

A primary key MUST be unique - that means, no two records can have the same value for their primary key. A good rule of thumb is to use an integer in this space that can be incremented by one each time. Alternatively, some databases may use a unique string for example an email address. This is appropriate as no two users will ever have the same email address. Some databases use a combination of surname and last name. For example Emma Banks would be Ebanks. However, numbers often make their way into these fields when you have two users finding themselves with the similar names. For example Emma Banks and Elliot Banks.

Composite Keys

A composite key is a primary key composed of multiple columns used to identify a record uniquely.
Below are a few real life examples that will help you understand what a key is: Conversation Excerpt:
Mum: “Who were you with today?”
Me: “A friend”
Mum: “Which friend?”
Me: “A friend from school”
Mum: “Which friend from school?”
Me :”A friend from my history class”
Mum:”Who?”
Me: “Jackson”
Mum: “Jackson who?”
Me: “Jackson Perreria”
Mum: “Oh, Jackson - Maria’s Son?”
Me:”Yup.”
Which piece of information do you believe was the primary key?
